Guide to Lanai's Beaches

Beach Guide · May 10, 2025 · 5 min read

Lanai has fewer beaches than Maui or Oahu, but what it lacks in quantity it makes up for in quality and solitude. These are beaches you can have largely to yourself — a rarity in modern Hawaii. Here's our definitive guide to the best ones.

Manele Bay / Hulopoe Beach

This is Lanai's most famous and accessible beach, and it deserves its reputation. A sweeping crescent of white sand backed by palms and lava rock, Hulopoe is consistently rated one of America's best beaches. The snorkeling here is world-class — the protected marine reserve supports an incredible diversity of tropical fish, sea turtles, and spinner dolphins. Access is easy from Lanai City (about 20 minutes by car), and facilities include restrooms and showers. The Four Seasons sits on the bluff above — non-guests are welcome on the public beach.

Shipwreck Beach

A long, windswept strand on Lanai's northern shore, Shipwreck Beach takes its name from the rusting hull of a WWII-era liberty ship visible offshore. This is not a swimming beach — the currents are dangerous — but it's extraordinary for walking, shell collecting, and photographing. Ancient Hawaiian petroglyphs are visible on the lava rock at the end of the beach. The drive there on dirt roads is part of the adventure.

Polihua Beach

Lanai's most remote beach requires a 4WD vehicle and is completely undeveloped — no facilities, no services, sometimes no other visitors. White sand backed by dunes, with sweeping views toward Molokai. Green sea turtles occasionally nest here. The 8-mile drive on rough dirt roads is worth every bump.

Kaununolu

Not a swimming beach but a fascinating archaeological site on Lanai's southern coast accessible by 4WD. The ancient Hawaiian fishing village here is one of the best-preserved in the state. Kamehameha the Great is said to have fished here. We can arrange guided visits for guests staying with us.
